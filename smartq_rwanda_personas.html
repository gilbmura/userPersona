Goals

Keep the lobby calm and organized, especially during the 8–10am rush
Know in advance how many clients are waiting so she can pace service calls
Reduce the number of clients who crowd the counter to check their turn
End each shift having served every client without clients leaving in frustration

Frustrations

Manually shouting ticket numbers causes confusion when the lobby is loud
No way to notify absent clients — skipped turns create disputes and backlog
Paper ticket rolls run out without warning; she must pause service to reprint
Supervisors ask for daily service counts but there is no automatic log

Environment

Busy urban office; up to 120 clients per day
Shared desktop PC at the counter; personal Android phone
Reliable office Wi-Fi; occasional power cuts

Key scenario

At 9 am on a Monday, Agathe faces 40+ people in the lobby. She tears ticket stubs and shouts numbers into the noise. Three clients have wandered outside; when their turn comes, they miss it and argue for re-entry. She spends 20 minutes resolving disputes instead of serving new clients.

"When there are more than thirty people here, I stop managing the queue — I'm just managing arguments. I need the system to call them so I can focus on actually helping."

Goals

Know roughly how long the wait will be before he even leaves his office
Receive a heads-up notification so he can walk in just before his turn
Avoid the anxiety of not knowing whether he will be served before closing time

Frustrations

Arrives early, waits 2+ hours with no indication of progress or estimated time
Must physically stay in the lobby to avoid losing his place in line
Crowded, stuffy waiting rooms — especially difficult after long commutes
Last visit: waited 3 hours only to be told the system was down; no warning given

Environment

Works 8–5; visits government offices during lunch or early morning
Android smartphone; uses MTN MoMo and WhatsApp daily
Commutes by moto-taxi; travel time to offices is 15–30 min

Key scenario

Emmanuel needs to renew his health insurance card. He arrives at 7:45 am to get a low ticket number, sits in an overcrowded waiting room, and misses a 10 am team meeting at work. He has no idea if he is 5 or 25 positions away. By 11 am he leaves unserved to make another critical work appointment, losing a full morning.

"I don't mind waiting — I mind not knowing. If my phone could just tell me 'you're next in 20 minutes,' I could actually plan my day around it."
